## Structure & Arrangement

### Song Structure

The recreation covers about 32 bars in length. There is a main drop of 16 bars and then a second half of that drop which is kind of like a verse. You would think it would maybe lead into another drop after this but it actually just leads into the outro. The overall structure of the song is quite a pop-like structure — a very radio-focused song structure where we have big sections of 16 bars and eight bars, with classic pop sections like a breakdown, verse, a buildup or pre-chorus, and then the chorus itself. It is designed to be played in a club but also designed to work very well on the radio where you have a very short space of time and need vocals to keep the listener interested.

### Tempo Change

Tempo changes are not very common in a lot of dance music genres. One of the reasons is that it can be a little bit of a problem for DJs to mix the song into their sets. The song starts out with a tempo of 136 beats per minute for the house section and then goes all the way up to 174 beats per minute for the drum and bass section at the end, creating a really interesting build and lift in energy. The tempo doesn't come back down at the end of the song either.

The tempo change seems to start about halfway through the big 16-bar breakdown section — approximately at bar 25 — so by the time you get to the actual buildup to the drum and bass section, you can already hear just a very slight increase in tempo, which gives it this sense of something big is about to change.

### Element Sequencing (Non-overlap)

The main piano chords and the arp synth play together at one point, and they have very contrasting rhythms which helps them stand out from each other. When we get into the drop, only the trumpet sound plays. Then when the trumpet stops playing, the arp starts to play. This helps keep the track nice and clean — not muddying up the track with too many sounds — because the vocal also lives on top of pretty much everything within the song.

## Key & Theory

### Key & Mode

The key of the track is G minor, but we start on a different note than the root note of the key. The first chord starts on the E flat, which is one to two steps below in terms of the scale — the root note G. That means it is in a Lydian key starting at that note two steps below, and this would be E flat Lydian, which is very similar to E flat major but just has a sharpened fourth. That sharpened fourth is the A natural. The track was written in G minor but all that was done is shifting down those two steps — a very nice way to create a new interesting chord sequence within a minor scale.

### Chord Progression

We have the following chords used in the sequence: E flat, F, G, B flat, C, and D at the bottom — which makes up six out of the seven chords. The only one that is missed is the A chord. The reason that chord is missed out is that when you are in a minor key like this, the note above your root note in the scale will be the diminished chord, and diminished chords just do not sound too great.

The last two chords lead back into the E flat at the beginning: C → D → E flat.

### Stretched Voicing

The main lead jumps between the first note of whatever chord is being played (the bass note) and the third note but an octave above where it would usually be within a standard triad. The root note of the chord is the E flat; the third note would generally be the G, but it is shifted up by an octave. This creates that really epic, anthemic feel to leads.

There is one chord that does differ from this: the second-to-last chord, which lands on the C. The root note is the C and the note above is a D — that is a major second interval instead of the third interval. The reason is likely that if you move that second interval up to the third, it lands back on the root note E flat. Having it on the third note along with all the other notes before it may make the sequence sound a little bit too happy — too much stability — so having it shifted down to the second note creates a bit more of a lift between the second-to-last chord and the last chord, helping to build tension before releasing back at the beginning.

### Diminished Chord Avoidance

In a minor key, the chord built on the note one step above the root of the minor key is the diminished chord. They just do not sound too great, and that is why that chord has been missed out. The sequence uses six of the seven available chords.

### Four-bar Melody Structure

The whole sequence is split into four-bar sections with the first two bars being the same in each four-bar section, and then the second half is the big difference. This is a great trick for writing any repetitive melody — just changing up the second half of a four-bar sequence gives the melody a little bit more longevity instead of repeating the exact same thing over and over again.

## Trumpet Lead

### Musical Role & Notes

The trumpet is the main lead sound in the song apart from the vocals. It is quite a simple melody — it just focuses around the stretched voicing idea. All that the pattern does is jump between the first note of whatever chord is being played (the bass note) and the third note an octave above. The only exception is a note like the F, which does not have that second note — it is more of a passing note. On the G, the third note is the B flat, but again just the octave above.

The second-to-last chord over here lands on the C with the note above being the D — a major second interval instead of the expected third.

### Patch (Serum)

Using Serum. Oscillator A and B are both on saw wave. Both have unison set to the maximum with some detune as well. Oscillator B is an octave above oscillator A — this helps add that brassy feel to the synthesizer.

Volume envelope: fast attack because it is quite a punchy brass stab. Sustain has been dropped down with a decay of 320 milliseconds and a little bit of a longer release so there is no instant stop of the sound.

Envelope 2 is assigned to the filter. The filter is very low — it cuts off quite a lot of those frequencies. The key part of this is that the attack is about 50 milliseconds, which provides that brassy stab at the beginning. No sustain, longest decay time.

### Effects & Processing

Heavy tube distortion — lots of heavy distortion to bring out those brassy tones. The multiband compressor is not used. EQ is used with some gain added at about 2,000 Hz with a high shelf. A filter at the end of the chain is set to the maximum filter setting in Serum — about 14k, 12 dB slope — just to roll off some of the very high frequencies. A lot of the low end has been taken out because it sounded like that in the mix and there was no desire for it to be too heavy or get in the way of the bass. Some high frequencies have been boosted. OTT is on after this at 23%. The filters are used for automation.

## Arpeggio Synth

### Pattern & Rhythm

In the first two bars, the pattern goes from highest note to the lowest to the one in between. When moving onto the next chord and specifically the one after that at bar three, the pattern changes to a regular down pattern. This is a bit of a strange thing — sometimes Ableton's arpegiator and Logic's arp can have an issue where starting one of the notes a little bit too late can cause weird things like this to happen.

The top note in each chord follows the melody of the trumpet pattern — that is the reason all the notes have been moved around and the pattern has changed slightly.

The chord sequences use three notes, which creates a syncopated rhythm because three sixteenth notes do not divide evenly into groups of four.

### Note Range Management

Using inversions and shifting notes up and down octaves helps keep a nice tight window in which the notes sit. The highest note is D4 and the lowest note is D2 — a two-octave window for all notes to live in. This means a lot less crossover with the low end of the bass, so they do not start to get in each other's way and become muddy.

### Chord Voicing vs Bass

When we get back to that G minor chord at the beginning, paired with the E flat on the bottom, the E flat gives the chord a completely new meaning. The root note of the G chord becomes the third, the B flat is the fifth, and the top note the D is a seventh note — providing a little bit of tension. From the third chord onwards there is more regular voicing with the G as the root note, the fifth in between, and the third note on top but an octave above.

I can imagine this is probably written on a piano, or just programmed in one note at a time, using the trumpet line as the top note and then placing all the other notes in so they fit around it.

## Piano & Pad Group

### Piano (Ableton Grand)

The piano chords are pretty simple — root note down on the bass with the stretched voicing where the third note has been moved up to the octave above, plus an additional fifth note on top. Nothing too crazy — more of a background layer to help harmonize the other instruments around them.

The melody above the chords is used a little bit in the first house section but is not used anywhere really after this point, or if it is, it is very very quiet in the mix. The rhythm is that syncopated rhythm where each note is a 3/16th note length (a dotted eighth note), and it resets every two bars.

Processing: auto filter for automation, a little bit of glue compression with a long attack and a medium release doing very little, just to control the punch and the tail.

### Organ Layer (Serum)

A Serum patch — fairly simple, kind of like an organ sound. Just a saw wave with the harmonic series on the third harmonic to bring out some more mid frequencies. Filter to cut off all of the low frequencies.

### Respace Layer (Diva)

Diva is used for variety and because Diva is very good at creating warm, low, subie bass sounds. Two oscillators turned on, both set to saw wave. One is detuned to minus 31 cents, the other to about plus 30 cents — equal and opposite instead of tuning just one up or down.

This bass sound is used in the breakdown as a layer underneath the pianos, and it was important not to make it too subie — if it is too subie it might detract from the drop later on. So when the filter cutoff is brought down, the resonance is brought up. On Pro Q3 this accentuates some of the upper harmonics more than the fundamental. A limiter was added after this to smooth out any very big volume jumps.

## Vocals

### Melody Shape & Pitch Trend

If you are writing any kind of vocals, this is a great technique: there is a trend of the notes slowly increasing in pitch as time goes on — starting very low in the breakdown, coming up to the middle point in the buildup, and then right at the end the highest notes of the vocal in the track. This provides a nice lift as you go into the chorus/drop.

### Interval Analysis — Breakdown

The base in the breakdown is a little bit different. Looking at the intervals with E flat as the main root note: start on the D (the seventh), then the sixth, fifth, fourth — all quite fast, so you do not really focus too much on the pitch. The main notes that are rested on are the B flat (fifth) and the G (third). When leading into the next chord, the bass note has changed, so the interval has changed too — the first half of the melody is the same but now the G to D is a fifth, fourth, third, second, third, and then we rest on the fourth note, which is a slightly tense note. You can hear it just creates a little bit of tension in the vocal during the breakdown section.

### Interval Analysis — Buildup

When we come into the next section, a big change in melody happens: E flat up to F (the second), then the sixth, and then the fifth that we rest on — more stability. Then the root note up here, F is first, root again (that would be the seventh interval), and then it moves up to the root note at the top for stability just before moving into the next section.

In the buildup the melody is much more simplified. E flat is the base note. The F here is the second, the D is the seventh note — tension in the first half which is then released in the second half. The fifth, a little bit of tension going from the seventh to the fifth, seventh, fifth, fifth, seven, four, fourth, and then back up to the fifth again — always landing on the last note of the fifth to help tame any interesting movement done before.

### Tension & Release Structure

The main melody in the song is a tale of two halves. The first half is really tense — it uses some fairly uncomfortable intervals to create tension that is then released in the second half. The root note of the base and the chords underneath is E flat, and these first three notes are actually the fourth, the second, and the seventh — all very tense intervals. A regular chord would have the first, third, and fifth, so the fourth, second, and seventh are all in between those.

The tension leads into the next chord with the G, because that is the third note within the chord. The real tension release comes in the section that is looped later in the main drop and in the buildup as well. In that section, G is the root note underneath everything. The fourth note on the top is a little bit tense, but then the third, and then a focus on the root note of G with big eighth notes. The base moving around underneath gives it loads of different harmonizations.

The vocal melody mainly uses minor pentatonic notes — the sequence does mainly use notes within the pentatonic scale. The A natural (the sharpened fourth) appears but is not rested on for too long, so it does not create too much tension. This applies across the breakdown, buildup, and drop sections.

## Bass

### Sub Bass Patch (Serum)

The subbase has a square wave creating odd-numbered harmonics and then a saw wave just blended in at a lower volume to help add a warm element to the bass. Turning off the saw wave sounds pretty nice, but turning it back on makes it sound a little bit thicker and fuller overall.

Filter: 24 dB, quite low cutoff. Envelope 2 is affecting the filter cutoff with a fast attack, 5-millisecond decay, and zero sustain. No other processing inside Serum.

EQ: a little bit of a boost at about 3 kHz to add some extra presence to the attack of the bass sound. OTT was added to bring out some of that extra punch, because this track is extremely compressed and limited.

### MIDI Note Length Effect

The MIDI effect before this is the note length MIDI effect. All it does is reduce the length of the notes. All notes are programmed as perfectly eighth-note length, but the note length effect is set to eighth notes and the gate is turned down to 83% of an eighth note — just shortening the notes. This helps create more of that stabby, rolling, percussive bass sound.

### Wide Bass Patch (Serum)

The wide bass sound is just receiving MIDI notes from the sub track above. It was spent quite a while on this sound — the main two characteristics were that it sounds plucky and wide. A saw wave and a square wave — the saw wave is an octave above. Both have unison turned on. White noise is layered into the sound for extra brightness to the pluck.

Filter: 18 dB, modulated by envelope 2 with no sustain and 700-millisecond decay. Envelope 1 is very similar — no sustain, 500–600 millisecond decay time.

Inside FX: EQ with some high frequencies boosted. A high pass filter up to about 72 Hz to reduce crossover between the sub bass and this bass — keeps the bass a little lighter without stacking lots of low frequencies on top of each other. OTT at 18% depth.

### MIDI Routing (Track Input)

Two separate tracks are used rather than an instrument rack. The wide track has its input set to the sub track above. Make sure it is on post effects if you have any MIDI effects too. Press in on the MIDI track and all that does is ignore the MIDI in that track itself and receive MIDI from the selected track. When the pattern in the sub part is changed, it also sends that MIDI to the wide part underneath. This saves a lot of time especially if instrument racks feel confusing. You can keep adding more tracks, each one selecting the input from the track above.

### Bass Group Processing

Both bass layers are grouped together with effects on the group to glue the two layers back together. Even if they are playing the same notes, they can sometimes sound like two layers of sounds rather than one overall bass sound.

On the group: Decapitator for saturation — it has a really nice tone compared to some of the stock Ableton saturators. It is not doing loads, but the distortion helps blend the two elements into one. OTT at 19% — very low. The reason for putting all these OTTs on is to bring up some of the higher frequencies; doing this inside an equalizer can sometimes sound a little bit harsh, so OTT is preferred for bringing those high frequencies up. EQ after this for an extra little bit of bite around 3K in the presence region.

A separate EQ in mid-side mode: cutting all of the low frequencies from around 250 Hz and below on the sides only — no wide bass signals. Finally, LFO Tool for sidechain linked just to the kick drum above. The final auto filter is just for automation.

## Drums

### Pattern

The drum pattern at the top is the classic standard drum and bass pattern: kick on the first beat, snare on the second, kick on the off beat in between beats three and four, and then snare on the fourth beat. That pattern just plays over and over again, with a few slight changes between different big sections of the song.

The high hat pattern mainly focuses around the eighth notes and the quarter notes — nothing too fancy, just playing on those eighth and quarter notes and accentuating them with the kick and the snare. It sounds like there is some kind of alternating clap that comes in on the second snare to layer on top, but it is extremely simple.

The bass also just plays an eighth-note pattern — often called a rolling baseline, used to make sure there is a very strong driving energy in the track. As soon as you start to add in other kick drums, snares, or bass notes that are not on those eighth notes, it starts to draw attention away from the other elements of the song, which are actually the more important elements in a track like this.

### Kick Processing

A lot of EQ work was done to get the kick to sound right and punchy enough. EQing to focus on the lower part of the kick, increasing some of that low-mid punch, and ducking some of the mid frequencies to get rid of a knocky effect. A boost up at about 2K — the slap of the kick drum. Very high 10K frequencies boosted too, because the kick in this track is extremely bright. Raw multiband distortion applied. Another EQ. Very very bright kick drum.

A little bit of compression — C XT comp — not doing a lot of gain reduction. 3:1 ratio, only two dB, but with a long attack and a fairly quick release, helping add a bit of extra punch at the beginning. This is used for automation. A soft clipper plugin is used: before the soft clipper the volume of the kick is 3.4 dB, and after, 3.4 dB again, but it sounds a lot louder because soft clipping is used to increase the volume back up.

### Snare Layers

Loads of different layers within one snare cell: a low acoustic snare from a sample vault providing the body; a higher-pitched, snappier snare sample; another extra snare sample providing crunch and more of the tail; and a Serum instance just adding white noise fizz to accentuate the tail as well.

A lot of EQ and distortion to bring the level up: EQ to increase a little bit of the low body (the fundamental frequency of the snare), cut some frequency in the middle (from the second, crunchier snare layer), tame some of the harsher frequencies, then boost the high frequencies again, and bring the overall volume back up using Decapitator. Then a bit of soft clipping.

## Buildup & Group Processing

All instruments are grouped together into a music group with a Valhalla Room reverb. During the buildup — about halfway through — the reverb mix is automated to go all the way up to 100%, so the musical elements drop further back into the mix as they do in the song. This is a great way of just removing some of the focus on those elements, and at that point the drums start to come in and the vocal plays too.

Soothe is used for sidechain dynamic EQing from the drum bus to the musical track group. All this does is create more separation between the drums and the musical elements.
