## General Features

## Drums

### Pattern Elements

- Starts with a huge Hard House kick — a big 909-style kick, tuned to the bassline so they fit well together. If you take it out of tune it doesn't punch as much, so always tune your kick.
- Main high hat and clap sitting right on top — pretty simple, but it's more about the sample selection. A really short high hat, not a big open high hat — a short punchy groove.
- Some house loops and a brake beat adding some high-end. They're all super high-passed and have a bit of autopan to sidechain them. Not really much mid-range in these, but they give a lot of energy and kind of blend with the other drums.
- A crash doing the classic house thing — every four bars or so. Being high-passed (very important with crashes because they can add a lot of low-end and mud to your mix). A little bit of reverb.

### Arrangement Notes

- If you didn't have the loops you would turn the main drums up a little louder, but when you hear them all together it kind of becomes one thing.

## Bassline

### Chord Progression & Notes

- Chord progression: F minor → A# minor → G# major → C# major → A# minor → G# major
- Root motion: root, 4, 3, 6, 4, 3
- There's a lot of symmetry to it. What makes it seem complicated is the jumping up to higher eighth notes — the best advice for that is to use notes you're already using. For example, over here is G# and then going to A then another G# up higher — it's not random notes, it's the same notes already used for the bass notes, just creating that pattern.
- It's an eighth-note feel — usually something that holds out for a while and then goes into that eighth-note feel.

### Patch (Wavetable)

- One oscillator of saw wave and a wavetable, plus a sub oscillator. The tones are all the way up on the sub oscillator.
- Low pass filter applied — it's a super tear-out Reese, then low-passed.
- Unison on.
- Because of the sub oscillator, you're still getting a kind of standard sub bass.
- Notch filtering: very good for that kind of Reese. Without it there's a bit too much mid-range. The original has a really hollow sort of UK bass kind of feel — the notch is the perfect way to do that, notching out all those mid-range frequencies and keeping the lows.
- A little bit of saturation.

### Processing

- Autopan to sidechain it to the kick.
- Converting the bass to mono with Utility.

## Lead

### Notes & Arrangement Role

- An eighth-note groove — really short, stabby notes. If you make the notes longer you lose it; it doesn't really feel like the original. It's not always just how you work with envelopes and synth design — a lot of the sound design happens in note length.
- In F minor, going on the third — already reacting with the original chord, really establishing the sad minor F. Then goes to root and fifth. Then the same thing but the fifth is just up an octave — sounds like different notes but it's actually just three notes the whole time. A lot of mileage out of just three notes.

### Patch (Analog)

- Made with Ableton Analog — could do this with a lot of different synths but for some reason the feel of Analog really matched what was going on in the original.
- Two saw waves [transcript: 'side waves'] detuned a little bit.
- No filter.
- Amp envelope like a brick — instant attack, instant release: as long as you play the note you hear it, the second you let go you stop hearing it.
- Unison — the Unison inside of Analog really fit. Without vs. with is clearly audible.
- Monophonic.

### Effects

- A little bit of reverb — short, high-passed.
- No autopan/sidechain on the lead. Had it on but ended up turning it down — you don't really hear the pattern that's in the original if you have too much of it. One element that doesn't have it. If you have too much of autopan you don't really hear that pattern.

## Deep House String

### Arrangement Role & Notes

- Playing the root note (F) as a high note, with a chord giving an octave down on top — just the note plus the note an octave down.

### Patch (Tension)

- Made with Ableton's Tension using the string Exciter to create a similar string sound.
- It's a bit more uneven than just using a saw, square, or sine wave, which gives it something nice — though you can't really get a really realistic string straight out of it.

### Effects

- A lot of reverb — that's where it really becomes a string sound. You can use reverb together with synthesis to create these kinds of deep house strings without just getting a sample or the M1 or whatever.
- Autopan — not that much sidechain but a little bit.
- High-passed — important because the reverb can add a lot of low-end.
